Pasta Dough

This is my favorite recipe to make Fettuccine (or any other pasta that is rolled out thinly). It is not all that much work, but it is as impressive as it is delicious. I make it for special occasions, to impress people, or to just have a darn good meal. This recipe is adapted from Helen Rennie's recipe.

    Makes 1 pound of pasta

  • 9 oz all-purpose flour (00 pasta and/or bread flour work as well, but are not necessary)
  • 1 tsp table salt
  • 5 to 5.5 oz liquid ingredients*
  • 2 tsp olive oil

    • *To make "liquid ingredients" weigh 2 egg yolks and 2-3 whole large eggs and add enough water to equal 5 oz total. If you went over, add more flour as necessary while kneading. When the air is very dry (like it is in the winter or in drier climates), increase liquid ingredients to 5.5 oz.

    If you do not like using only egg yolks, you can simply use 3-4 whole eggs instead. I have done both and have not noticed a big difference. (given, I have not had them side by side)

  1. Measure flour into a bowl, add salt. Mix salt and flour evenly. Create a “well” in the middle of dry ingredients. Then add wet ingredients into the dry ingredient “well”. Add olive oil. Beat together until a shaggy dough is formed, then knead**.

    2. **The recommended kneading technique is the French Fold. This creates the best/most consistent gluten development.

  2. Knead for 8-12 minutes, add flour as necessary to hands and/or work surface. Very often, the proportions of the dough are right, but they feel wrong at first (i.e. “this dough is way too wet I need a lot more flour”). The dough will usually come together with some kneading as long as the recipe is followed.
  3. Lightly cover in flour and wrap in Saran Wrap or a ziplock bag. Let it rest at room temperature for 20-40 minutes. (longer is ok, but make sure the dough is well covered so it does not dry out)
  4. Cut the dough into manageable pieces (about 1/4ths or 1/6ths work well), and roll out to setting 4-6 on a KitchenAid pasta dough attachment or about 1/8” thick.
  5. After dough is rolled out, be sure to flour well and allow to “dry” while you roll the rest of the dough. Once in a while, flipping each sheet of dough to dry evenly. Desired texture before cutting is like a rough leather.

    6. If you would like to add something special to your dough, say roll in a few leaves of basil, do that here. You can roll out your dough to setting ~2 on a KitchenAid pasta attachment (maybe 3/16") and "laminate" your basil leaves, or other accoutrements by folding the dough in half onto itself once. Continue rolling from here.

  6. Then cut with a knife or desired shape pasta attachment. Flour the uncut and cut dough well so it does not stick or clump together. Work from first rolled to last rolled.
  7. Meanwhile, boil a large pot of water with plenty (1 to 1.5 tsp per quart of water) of salt to increase the boiling point of the water. (This step does not do much to impart a salty flavor into the dough as it cooks for a short time, we have already added the salt for taste. If the dough is too salty, use less salt in the dough, but continue to salt the water well. By increasing the boiling point, the pasta will take less time to cook.)
  8. Stir the water while you add your pasta (this step is easier with more than 1 person). Stir and continue to cook the pasta in the water on high heat for 1 minute and 30 seconds, up to about 2 minutes. Test for doneness after about a minute of cooking.
  9. Serves well with butter, olive oil, or a sauce of your choosing (or even plain if you can eat it quickly). Non-buttered/oiled/sauced pasta will clump quickly after it is removed from the water.
