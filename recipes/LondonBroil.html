London Broil

Cooking and serving steaks for a large group of people is difficult and tiresome (not to mention it can get expensive). Grilling (or broiling) one or two large roast(s) for the same size group is easier, cheaper, more efficient, and sometimes just as delicious!
This is a recipe for one such roast with a sauce that is to die for. The cut of beef is cheap and easy to find. And to top it all off, it eats well in any season of the year.
Enter, the London Broil (grilled).

  • 2-3 lbs top round roast (sometimes labeled “London Broil”) or flank steak
  • 1 cup beef stock (low sodium preferred; can sub water, but use half of a cup)
  • 2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 3 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp teriyaki sauce
  • 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tbsp balsamic vinegar
  • 1 lemon zested and juiced
  • 3 large cloves of garlic minced
  • 1/2 tsp cayenne pepper or ground red pepper
  • 1/2 tsp crushed red pepper flakes
  • 1 tbsp extra virgin olive oil
  • Kosher salt
  • Fresh ground black pepper
  1. In a medium bowl add all ingredients, leaving the roast and oil last. Mix well and taste for salt and black pepper. Add them as necessary, but the marinade should have nearly enough salt from the soy sauce and other ingredients.
  2. Next while mixing the marinade, slowly pour the olive oil in.
  3. Dry the roast of any liquid from packaging. Tenderize with a needle tenderizer, or poke with a thin knife in many spots, or lightly use a mallet tenderizer.
  4. Add the roast to a container, ziplock freezer bag, or a vacuum sealed bag. Add the marinade to the container or bag after mixing again.
  5. Place in refrigerator for 6 to 30 hours.
  6. Remove roast from marinade, and reserve the marinade.
  7. Place the roast on a medium-high heat grill. Alternatively if you want to keep with the name, place the roast on a sheet pan and into an oven set to broil ensuring at least 3 inches between the top of the roast and the heat source.
  8. Broil the roast for 10-15 minutes in the oven before flipping (aim for some good browning on each side) and broiling for another 10-15 minutes (or until the internal temperature hits 130* f). On the grill, cook each side for about 4-6+ minutes depending on thickness and browning (or until the internal temperature hits 130* f). You can flip the rost(s) a couple of times here or reposition on each side to get attractive grill marks.
  9. Meanwhile, pour marinade into a small pot or saucepan or saucier and bring to a boil. Reduce heat to a simmer and allow the liquid to reduce.
  10. Once the liquid is about 2/3rds of its original volume, add butter to another small pot. Melt it and add flour while whisking to create a roux. Allow roux to cook about 30 seconds, still whisking (careful not to burn anything here).
  11. Finally, slowly pour the marinade reduction into the roux to create a gravy-like sauce. Add salt and/or freshly ground black pepper as necessary.
  12. Once the roast hits an internal temperature of 130* f in either method, remove from heat and allow to rest. The meat will continue to cook slightly, a solid medium doneness is likely the end result which will satisfy medium rare lovers and well done crazies alike (as long as they are a bit open minded). With a tough cut like this, that is a good target to hit.
  13. Slice no more than 1/4 inch thick AGAINST the grain (as with any meat, but especially with a particularly tough cut such as this). If the roast is too long to handily slice in one piece, cut the roast in half or thirds with the grain so you can then cut against the grain on the smaller halves or thirds.
  14. Serve the slices with sauce ladled over them, with sauce on the side, or smother them in the sauce.
